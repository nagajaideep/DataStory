Emotional Chatbot: The Indian Data Story

A comprehensive guide to building culturally calibrated emotional AI. From Selecting the right LLM to sourcing specialized Indian conversational datasets.

12 min read · By Anshap Research Team

1. The Problem Space

Emotional AI chatbots sit at the intersection of NLP, psychology, and cultural intelligence. Most existing models are trained on Western, English-first datasets, making them poor fits for Indian users who express emotions through code-mixed language (Hinglish), culturally specific idioms, and different norms around vulnerability and mental health-seeking.

Building a robust emotional chatbot for an Indian audience means solving three layered problems: the right base model, the right datasets, and cultural calibration.

2. Which LLM Should You Use?

Closed / API Models (Rapid Prototyping)

  • Claude Sonnet 3.5 / Opus 3 — Best for empathetic tone: Claude brings unmatched clarity in communication and emotionally intelligent writing. It consistently ranks as the most "human-feeling" model.
  • GPT-4o — Best overall empathy balance: In head-to-head tests, GPT-4o led in reading underlying distress and immediate safety checks on self-harm prompts.
  • Grok 1.5 — Highest EQ benchmark score: Grok currently holds a top spot on EQ-Bench, measuring ability to understand subtext and social cues.
  • Gemini 1.5 Pro — Best for multimodal emotional understanding: Useful if you later want voice or video emotion input.

Open Source / Self-Hostable (Fine-tuning on Indian data)

  • LLaMA 3 (Meta): The most fine-tuning-friendly open model. Default starting point for custom emotional chatbots using LoRA.
  • Mistral Large / Mixtral: Good mid-range options with privacy-sensitive deployment capabilities.
  • DeepSeek V2: Cost-efficient and strong for low-resource fine-tuning scenarios.
Recommendation: Use Claude Sonnet 3.5 or GPT-4o for the base API (warmest tone, best safety guardrails), or LLaMA 3 fine-tuned with LoRA if you want full data control and Indian-language customization.

3. Emotional Datasets — Global Foundation

EmpatheticDialogues

25,000 human-to-human dialogues grounded in 32 emotion labels. The benchmark for Empathetic Response Generation.

ESConv

Emotional Support Conversation dataset. Models how a supporter helps someone navigate emotional distress over multiple turns.

IEMOCAP

Multimodal dataset covering anger, happiness, sadness, frustration, and neutral states.

CounselChat / PsyQA

Datasets constructed from counselor-patient interactions, used for training on therapeutic dialogue.

4. Indian & South Asian Datasets

Localization is critical. These datasets capture the specific emotional nuances of the Indian context:

  • EmoInHindi (IIT Patna): 44,247 utterances for mental health and legal counselling, annotated with 16 emotion categories.
  • MEISD (IIT Patna): Multimodal Multi-Label Emotion, Intensity and Sentiment Dialogue Dataset.
  • EmoMent: Emotion-annotated mental health corpus from South Asian public Facebook groups.
  • Hindi Conversational Mental Health: Adapted from Empathetic Dialogues for counselor-like responses in Hindi.
  • Hinglish Code-Mixed Tweets: Realistic representation of how Indian users express emotion online (Hindi-English mix).

5. Psychologist & Clinical Data Sources

Grounded in actual therapeutic frameworks:

  • CounselChat: Real Q&A between counselors and clients.
  • PARTNER (IIT Patna): Persuasive mental health dialogue system for women and children crime victims.
  • PSY framework datasets: Built around CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy) and motivational interviewing strategies.

Benchmark Insight: While Grok leads in raw EQ score, Claude 3 remains the favorite for clinical applications due to its higher "Warmth" and "Tact" scores on the HEART benchmark.
